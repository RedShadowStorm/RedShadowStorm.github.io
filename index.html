Parachutes

Purpose Variables Hypothesis Materials Procedure Observations Results Conclusion

Purpose

The purpose of our experiment was to examine which parachute shape would be the best. Our “best” is not black and white, seeing that some people need fast fall velocities, (though that velocity should not be enough to kill them) for the thrill of falling at high speeds. There is also the factor of how accurate of how accurately a parachute can take its payload to its intended target.

Variables

We must keep the surface area of every parachute canopy we will be using, we will also keep the number of strings attached to every parachute constant (8 strings) as well so that equal amounts of force are exerted on every parachute canopy. We also used the exact same 20 gram weight for every trial of the parachutes. The independent variables we will be changing are the shape of the parachute and the string length of the parachute and the regularity of the shapes we are using. We will not be changing the heights of which we are dropping the parachute from. The dependant variables are the fall times and fall velocities of each parachute as well as the stability of the fall (how straight the fall is).

Hypothesis

We expect that circles will provide the slowest fall velocity because they spread the air out equally around the entire parachute canopy because they have no angles because they have no straight sides. We also expect that all the regular shaped parachute canopies will provide more of a stable fall (more accurate) with less diagonal fall.

Observations

We observed that the slower the fall velocity the more unstable the parachute (going off target to places that were far off from the area, crashing into walls where it would be expected to land).This because too much drag is created so more upwards force is being applied on the parachute leading the parachute and its payload away from the predicted landing trajectory. We also observed that after every test the strings attaching the parachute canopy to the payload got tangled, and we had to untangle them so that the parachute (on the next trial) would not go spinning off into the wall. A trend we noticed is that the more sides the shape had the slower fall velocity. The only shape this is not true to that even though the circle has the most sides, it did have a faster fall velocity than the Octagon and Hexagon. We also observed that on the shapes with fewer sides when we put the strings on they were a further distance from the centre than for the canopies with more sides (ex: Octagon and Circle).

Conclusion

Our experiment supported our Hypothesis in the fact that the shapes with more sides had slower fall velocities, but this was not true if the parachute shape is a circle. We conclude that the Octagon has the slowest fall velocity as a parachute shape. This is because it catches more air on the way down and causing it to have more lift. We also found that it is very important. Being that catching air is equal to more drag and, drag is the force parachutes rely on to slow themselves down with. When there is too much drag, our parachutes would go off target and slam into the walls. As a next step we could also experiment about if varying the string length of a parachute would have any effect as well as testing if having vents on the Parachute canopy.
